Saturday morning
The screen unrolled an endless strip of seafloor in burnt orange tones--grainy sand ripples and dark blotches of coral sliding past like an old photograph. For the past hour, the images had been featureless, the monotony broken only by the occasional lobster pot.
Then, slowly, a shape emerged. Long. Cylindrical. Winged.
Evan sat forward, eyes narrowing. "What the hell is that?"
Sofia traced the outline with her finger. "Looks like a plane. But what's it doing down there?"
Carter gave a low whistle, arms crossed as he leaned against the cabin bulkhead. "Just our luck. We chase the Carmen and trip over a modern wreck."
"another wreck hunter would kill for this find... but it's not the prize we came for." 

On the glowing orange strip, the fuselage seemed eerily intact, the wings sharp against the seafloor.
"Can we dive it? If we have to be here on Saturday hunting for the mythical Carmen, at least we can get a dive out of it." Evan asked.
"Finish the grid first," Sofia said, pragmatic as always. "If there's time, we'll take a look. And you know Papá has his precious log book, and that is why we are looking for the Carmen here."
Evan rolled his eyes. Everyone on the Treasure Coast had stories about the 1715 fleet, but only their family had a leather-bound log said to come from a survivor. Papá claimed it held sketches and notes passed down from a sailor who swore he'd seen the Carmen turn back. Whether it was real history or family folklore, no one outside the Ramirezes had ever laid eyes on it.

Evan rolls eyes, goes back to watching the sonar screen, putting a marker on the GPS location of the plane crash as it drifted off the edge of the screen, leaving only sand and coral again--like a ghost daring them to turn back.

Tone Guide - Lost Legacy Series
Core Flavor
   •	High-stakes adventure + modern thriller realism.
	•		•	Characters are hyper-competent but never grimdark.
	•	The crew faces danger with confidence, resourcefulness, and wit.

Principles
1. Always Capable
	•	The Ramirezes and allies are professionals -- Navy SEAL training, FBI background, treasure-hunting expertise.
	•	Even when things go wrong, readers trust they can think/act their way out.
	•	Tone check: The thrill comes not from if they'll survive, but how cleverly they'll turn the tables.
2. Humor Under Pressure
	•	Jokes, banter, and playful digs are part of their survival style.
	•	Quips cut tension, but never undercut the danger.
	•	Example: Carter joking about "Shark Week" while literally fending off sharks.
3. Running Gags & Callbacks
	•	Recurring motifs (e.g., "always need a Plan B") build continuity and fan recognition.
	•	References to SEAL movies, pop culture, and personal quirks create familiarity and comic relief.
4. Family Dynamics First
	•	Banter isn't just for laughs -- it reveals relationships (Evan + Carter brotherly ribbing, Sofia's sharp comebacks, Marty's dry wit).
	•	Characters argue, tease, and protect each other like family -- this is the emotional heart of the tone.
5. Contrast Is Key
	•	Environment: exotic wrecks, grim warehouses, luxury yachts → always vivid, cinematic.
	•	Tone: danger is serious, but the characters stay light on their feet. That contrast is what makes the series fun.
6. Clarity & Accessibility (Patterson-style)
	•	Keep prose lean, cinematic, and always moving.
	•	No overlong description or technical digression -- every page should flow as fast as dialogue.
7. Mystery Breadcrumbs (Brown-style)
	•	Each story must contain at least one puzzle/conspiracy thread (cult lore, treasure history, hidden code).
	•	Readers should feel they're uncovering clues alongside the crew.
8. Emotional Anchors (Baldacci-style)
	•	Each book must include at least one moment where the stakes hit below the treasure.
	•	Emotional risks (family, trust, legacy, love) deepen the adventure.
	•	Examples within Ravi's Treasure:
	◦	Sofia worries about Pops' health as he pushes them into danger.
	◦	Sofia suspects Evan might be hiding something after the Salvage Dive -- tension of betrayal under the adventure.
	◦	Epilogue beat: Sofia imagines Pops finally resting by the sea, giving the story a quiet emotional payoff.

Tone Benchmarks
Burn Notice
	•	Competence + Humor: The Ramirezes face danger like Michael Westen's crew -- always capable, always clever, but never too serious to crack a joke mid-mission.
	•	Family Banter as Coping: Like Michael, Fiona, and Sam, humor isn't fluff -- it's how the crew handles life-or-death tension.
	•	Improvised Plans: Resourcefulness and on-the-fly problem solving keep the story grounded (Plan B is always in play).
	•	Stylish Danger: Violence and threat are real, but the tone stays sharp, witty, and character-driven rather than grimdark.
Other Benchmark Examples
	•	Mission Impossible → team competence + playful personality clashes.
	•	Leverage → family-style banter + clever cons.
	•	Early Clive Cussler → adventure pulp with wit and energy.

Above the cash sat a black duffel bag, half-zipped, bulging with bundles. On the shelves above were cases, folders, and odd treasures, the hoard of a paranoid magpie.
Sofia's eyes caught on a glossy blue box. She slid it down -- Tiffany & Co., the ribbon yellowed with age. She eased the lid open.
Inside, a diamond necklace blazed against faded velvet, extravagant beyond belief.

Marty: "That was the Countess di Caravaggio's necklace. Stolen in Florence in the '60s. Worth ten million dollars. If it's here, it means the rumors are true -- Guru Ravi Omkar was working with the School of Turin."

The PoisonMarty tugged a manila folder from the shelf, flipping it open. His face darkened.

Marty shook his head.

Sofia's chest tightened. She thought of Antonio -- her father, who once only reported part of a treasure haul to the U.S. government. She wasn't supposed to know, but she did: he'd stashed the rest in deposit boxes all over town. Gold. Coins. Secrets. Paranoia ran in families.
She closed the locker door with a firm hand -- not locked, just so Bain couldn't see in.
Sofia: "I have an idea."
She stepped over and pressed the red button.

The ProposalEverett returned, hands folded.
Everett: "Did you find everything in order?"
Sofia smiled, steady.
"Yes. And we'd like to rent two lockers of our own. Today."

Before Sofia could falter, Marty leaned in smoothly.

The SplitThe paperwork was brisk, signatures scrawled, crisp bills peeled from Ravi's own hoard to cover the fees and bonus. Everett left them alone.
They worked quickly.
- Marty took his share -- a quarter of the cash, a felt bag heavy with diamonds, and a handful of bearer bonds -- zipped into his new locker.
- The rest Sofia shoveled into hers: bricks of cash, two bags of diamonds, the bulk of the bonds.
They moved with silent urgency, knowing time was short.
What they left: a couple hundred thousand in loose bills, the duffel with flashy stolen jewelry, the blackmail files, and the Turin reliquary in its case. Enough to make Ravi's box look untouched -- and poisonous to whoever tried to claim it.

ExitSofia zipped her backpack -- heavy now, with another folded inside -- and pressed the red button again.
When Everett returned to escort them out, he found nothing but two polite new clients, calm and empty-handed.
But inside, Sofia's heart was racing. She carried a fortune on her back, and a plan already forming.

Nashua, Bahamas

History of the Bahamas
Early Peoples
	•	The first known inhabitants were the Lucayan Taíno, arriving around 600-800 AD from the Caribbean. They lived by fishing, farming, and trading.
	•	When Columbus made his first landfall in 1492 (on an island he named San Salvador), the Lucayans were enslaved and transported, and within a generation, they were gone.
Colonial Era
   •	The islands were mostly uninhabited in the 1500s-1600s, becoming havens for pirates and privateers.
	•	In 1648, English Puritans called the Eleutheran Adventurers settled on Eleuthera, beginning permanent European colonization.
	•	The Bahamas became a British Crown colony in 1718, when the British Navy expelled most pirates.
Pirate Age & Treasure Lore
      •	Nassau was once the infamous "Republic of Pirates" in the early 1700s, run by figures like Blackbeard (Edward Teach) and "Calico Jack" Rackham.
      •	Pirate treasure myths endure:
	   ◦	Blackbeard's Treasure is rumored to be hidden in the Bahamas, though never found.
	   ◦	Henry Avery and Charles Vane also operated in Bahamian waters; legends suggest caches of plunder buried on remote cays.
	◦		◦	Many shipwrecks from Spanish treasure fleets (gold, silver, emeralds from the Americas) litter the surrounding reefs, fueling ongoing salvage expeditions.
Modern Era
	•	The Bahamas gained independence from Britain in 1973, though it remains a Commonwealth nation with the British monarch as head of state.
	•	 Today it is politically stable, English-speaking, and highly dependent on tourism and offshore banking.

Offshore Banking & Secrecy
	•.  Since the 1960s, the Bahamas has been known for banking secrecy laws and as a hub for offshore finance.
	•.  In the 1980s-1990s, it was popular for shell companies, tax avoidance, and discreet deposits.
	•	International pressure (FATF, OECD) forced reforms in the 2000s, requiring more transparency and cooperation with foreign regulators.
	•	Still, private banks and trust companies in Nassau and Freeport handle billions in assets. Safety deposit boxes, numbered accounts, and offshore trusts remain central to its allure.
	•		•	Lore for fiction:
	◦	Old institutions may still honor "bearer key = owner" style access for vaults or deposit boxes (no ID required), though this is no longer legal in reputable banks.
	◦	Small family-owned banks or discreet private vault services on out islands could plausibly bend the rules--perfect for a thriller setup.

The Bahamas in Popular Treasure Lore
	•. The Spanish Treasure Fleet of 1715 wrecked in a hurricane off Florida's coast, scattering gold and silver into Bahamian waters. Many wrecks remain undiscovered.
	•. Stories circulate about sunken galleons in the Tongue of the Ocean (a deep trench between Nassau and Andros).
	•. In modern pop culture, the Bahamas is often linked to the Bermuda Triangle--a nearby zone of supposed vanishings. Treasure, cults, and mysterious disappearances fit naturally into this legend-rich backdrop.

The 1715 Hurricane
   •	It struck the fleet just after they left Havana, around July 30-31.
   •	Contemporary accounts are sparse, but most agree the storm came out of the east-southeast, a classic Cape Verde-type hurricane track.
	•	Instead of curving north into the open Atlantic, it barreled straight west-northwest, slamming into Florida's coast near modern Fort Pierce.
Survivor Accounts
   •	Survivors reported ships driven "onto the reefs of Florida, dashed to pieces by the seas."
   •	They set up makeshift camps along the coast, which the Spanish used as salvage bases for years.
	•	There's little mention of ships heading east -- which makes sense, because:
	◦	In those conditions, sailors would have tried to stay close to land (Florida coast) rather than risk being swept into the deep Atlantic.
	◦		◦	But if one ship (like the Carmen) was disabled, lost masts, or tried to run before the wind, it could have been caught by the north-flowing Gulf Stream and pulled away from the coast.
Why the Carmen Could Be Different
   •	If overloaded with treasure and riding lower in the water, she would have been more vulnerable.
   •	If her captain decided Florida's reefs were certain death, steering southeast into the Bahamas channel might have seemed the only chance -- even though it meant running with the storm.
	•	That gamble would explain why no wreck was ever logged on Florida's coast for her.
The Lost Galleon: Nuestra Señora del Carmen
   •	Part of the 1715 Treasure Fleet, the Carmen was a medium-sized galleon heavily loaded with silver reales, gold ingots, and emeralds from Colombia.
   •	When the hurricane struck on July 31, 1715, most ships were driven onto Florida's reefs. But some survivors' accounts mention a galleon that broke formation and vanished into the storm.
	•	Spanish salvage records are strangely silent on the Carmen's fate. No recovery manifests mention her cargo. Historians assume she went down and was salvaged quietly -- but no confirmed wreck has ever been tied to her.

Ramirez Family Lore
	•	    •	Immigration Background: Antonio's parents, Diego and Marisol Ramirez, immigrated to Florida from Galicia, Spain, in 1947, part of the wave of post-WWII migrants seeking stability and opportunity. They brought little more than a trunk of possessions, a family Bible, and a battered leather logbook that had passed down through generations.
	•	   •	The Logbook's Origins: The logbook claimed descent from Mateo de la Vega, a young sailor who survived the wrecks of the 1715 Treasure Fleet. Family lore held that Mateo had been aboard the Nuestra Señora del Carmen when a hurricane broke the fleet apart. The Carmen lagged behind, and in the chaos, Mateo claimed to have seen her turn back south before vanishing into the storm. He washed ashore near Havana clinging to wreckage, carrying a fragment of the Carmen's ship's log that he bound into his own journal.
	•		•	Secrecy & Legacy: Passed father to son, the logbook became a mixture of facts and whispers--currents, sketches of reefs, notes on tides, coded family marks. Each generation added observations from fishing voyages or coastal life in Spain. Diego showed it to Antonio only once, saying: "Our family came here because of this. Don't ever forget the sea owes us a debt."
	•	Antonio's Obsession: Antonio grew up leafing through the faded pages by lamplight. Even after becoming a professional salvor, he believed the Carmen wasn't just a myth but a solvable puzzle--her name carved into the family's destiny. His meticulous dive logs are modeled on the family book, sometimes even blending the two so outsiders can't tell where the lore ends and his research begins.
	•	Themes for Later Books:
	◦	Rivals dismiss Antonio's "ghost maps" as superstition, but they contain subtle truths about currents and storm tracks.
	◦	The family logbook is stolen, destroyed, or revealed in Book 4, forcing his children to reconstruct its contents from memory.
	◦	Mateo's survivor tale may tie directly into an undiscovered cache--providing the real key to finding the Carmen.
