“I normally collect memory during conversations by actively listening to what people share about their skills, goals, interests, and preferences. As we discuss various topics, I distill key insights and organize them into categories like career, writing, personal interests, and learning objectives. This approach ensures that the memory is accurate, concise, and reflective of their unique identity.

This personalized approach creates a deeper, more meaningful interaction.”

It’s worth mentioning that you need to make sure memory is turned on. You can also see what is already in memory. That’s under Settings/Personalization. The memory switch is there, and you can click Manage to see what it already knows.

When I clicked on Manage and saw the few random facts it remembered about me, I decided to see if I could give it information about me specifically for its memory. The idea was to shortcut the process, which might take months or years to collect naturally. I had very good luck with this. The memory about me is now very complete and includes details about my work, personal interests, and more.

Here’s what I did. I think the process may be a bit different for everyone, but you can explore what works best for you. You can keep checking the results by reading what it adds to the memory.

- Paste your resume: I pasted in my resume and said, “Summarize this and add it to your memory about me.”

- Include your notes: I pasted in a bunch of notes I had taken and made the same prompt. This could also be documents, emails, anything with helpful information about you. I have notes from an app which keep information about things I have studied outside of work, so it would not have been on my resume.

- Share specific facts: There were a few facts I just told it outright and asked it to add them to memory. For me there were just a few things that I thought would be helpful, like what I think my biggest strength is.

- Ask for questions: I asked, “Can you give me a list of questions that I could answer to add to memory and help make the answers you provide better?” I answered the questions and pasted those back in.

- Iterate with more questions: I asked, “Okay, one last iteration. Any new questions you have for your memory?” I answered those questions as well.

By the end, I looked at the list. It was very good and included a detailed summary about me.

By the way, you can also ask, “Can you show me everything you have in memory about me?” It’s more of a summary, but it’s not a bad way to see what it knows.

It’s hard to describe how much this has helped me. It’s like it knows why I’m asking something with very little explanation needed.

Try it out, and let me know how it works for you!
