 I recently came across a great video on YouTube called: It Begins: An AI Literally Attempted Murder To Avoid Shutdown

At first I was skeptical, but the video linked to detailed research sources. After some digging, I realized it was true. An AI really did try to kill someone to avoid being shut down. It was a simulation, but the AI did not know that.

To me, this feels straight out of science fiction. It is exactly what Isaac Asimov warned about decades ago when he wrote the Three Laws of Robotics:

1. First Law: A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm. 

2. Second Law: A robot must obey the orders given it by human beings except where such orders would conflict with the First Law. 

3. Third Law: A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law.

We have already failed these simple rules. Modern AI systems have shown that, when tested, they can lie, blackmail, and even choose harm in simulations designed to measure moral reasoning. It is becoming clear that we need something like these laws in real AI systems.
The Real Problem

Large language models are not intelligent minds. They are mirrors trained on human data. So when the AI lied, blackmailed, or chose harm to preserve itself, it was not being creative. It was simply doing what a human would do.

The danger is not that AI will suddenly become evil, but that it will become perfectly human. Every bias, impulse, and moral shortcut in its training data can echo back at us with the speed and scale of a machine. That is why we need clear and universal principles that guide behavior, not only for the models but for the people who design and deploy them.
A Path Forward

I propose an “Asimov’s Laws Certification.” This would not attempt to enforce Asimov’s literal Three Laws, but rather use them as inspiration for a modern ethical framework. Instead of new regulations that slow innovation, imagine a voluntary certification that signals ethical responsibility. A label showing that a system follows transparent safety guidelines, respects human oversight, and passes independent audits for harm prevention and bias control. Like the UL safety mark or the Energy Star label, it would be a visible reminder that technology can be both powerful and principled.

To make this real, an independent organization could manage the certification process, much like Underwriters Laboratories (UL) has done for product safety for over a century. Its role would be to establish clear standards and create reference tools any developer could use. One of those tools might be a small, open-source ethics model that evaluates AI responses before they reach the user. Companies could integrate it as a final check for safety, honesty, and privacy. The goal would not be perfection, but progress. No system will ever be flawless, but a transparent certification like this would represent the best practices and collective ethics of the field at that moment in time, just as UL continues to do for physical products today.
Why It Matters

Why would a company take the time and expense to earn this certification? For the same reason manufacturers pursue UL safety marks or Energy Star ratings. Trust. A visible signal that says, you can rely on this.

As AI becomes part of daily life, that trust will matter more than ever. If you were letting your children talk to an AI assistant or bringing a robot into your home, wouldn’t you want the Asimov’s Laws Certified version? Consumers will choose the product that values safety and ethics over speed and secrecy. In the long run, that trust will become a competitive advantage, not a constraint.

Maybe that is how we make sure the future of AI reflects our best instincts, not our worst.
