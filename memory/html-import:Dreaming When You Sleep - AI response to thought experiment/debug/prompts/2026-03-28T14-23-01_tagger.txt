It was a simulation, but the AI didn’t know that.

To me, this is straight out of science fiction. It is exactly what Isaac Asimov predicted decades ago, leading him to write a series of stories that proposed these laws:
The Three Laws of Robotics
1. First Law: A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.
2. Second Law: A robot must obey the orders given it by human beings except where such orders would conflict with the First Law.
3. Third Law: A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Law.

It is becoming clear that we need to implement something like these laws in real AI systems. But how?
Imagine seeing “Asimov’s Laws Certified” printed next to “UL Listed” or “Energy Star” on an AI product.

I hate to say this, but large language models are not intelligent minds. They are mirrors trained on human data. So when the AI lied, blackmailed, or chose harm to preserve itself, it was not being creative. It was just doing what a human would do.

The danger is not that AI will suddenly become evil, but that it will become perfectly human. Every bias, impulse, and moral shortcut in its training data can echo back at us with the speed and scale of a machine. That is why we need clear and universal principles that govern behavior, not just for the models but for the people who design and deploy them.

To accomplish this I propose an “Asimov’s Laws Certification”. Instead of new regulations that stifle innovation, imagine a voluntary certification that signals ethical responsibility. A label that tells users this system follows transparent safety guidelines, obeys human oversight, and passes independent audits for harm prevention and bias control. Like the UL safety mark or the Energy Star label, it would be a visible reminder that technology can be both powerful and principled.

To make this real, an independent organization could oversee the certification process, much like Underwriters Laboratories (UL) has done for product safety for over a century. Its role would be to establish clear standards and provide reference tools that any developer can use. One of those tools might be a small, open-source ethics model that evaluates AI responses before they reach the user. Companies could integrate it into their systems as a final check for safety, honesty, and respect for privacy. The goal would not be perfection, but progress. No system will ever be flawless, but a transparent certification like this would represent the best practices and collective ethics of the field at that moment in time. Just as UL continues to do for physical products today.

Why would a company take the time and expense to earn this certification? For the same reason manufacturers pursue UL safety marks or Energy Star ratings. Trust. A visible signal that says, “You can rely on this.” As AI becomes part of daily life, that trust will matter more than ever. If you were letting your children talk to an AI assistant or bringing a robot into your home, wouldn’t you want the Asimov’s Laws Certified version? Consumers will choose the product that shows it values safety and ethics over speed and secrecy. In the long run, that trust will become a competitive advantage, not a constraint.
