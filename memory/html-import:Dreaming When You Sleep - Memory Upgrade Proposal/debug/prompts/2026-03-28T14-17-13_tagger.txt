Future Upgrade: Memory Strength via Importance and Stability, with Repetition Reinforcement and Dedup Consolidation
Problem this upgrade solves
. Current pruning reduces prompt size by removing extraneous conversation text, but it does not prevent long-term memory bloat. . Over time, the same ideas, decisions, and themes reappear many times, creating duplicated memories. . Without a dedup and consolidation mechanism, LTM becomes: . Larger than needed. . Harder to retrieve from (noise increases). . More expensive to maintain. . More likely to surface outdated or redundant variants of the same idea.
Core concept
Introduce explicit, numeric memory persistence controls that mimic biological memory selection:
. importance ∈ [0, 1]: should this memory exist in long-term storage at all. . stability ∈ [0, 1]: how resistant this memory is to revision, summarization, and deletion. . Keep: human override flag that prevents deletion (equivalent to forcing importance and stability to maximum plus a “never delete” rule).
This maps to a two-stage brain-like process: . Online or light sleep: fast “bookmarking” tags important events (like awake sharp-wave ripple tagging). . Deep sleep: slower consolidation selects, merges, stabilizes, and prunes (like sleep replay consolidation).
Data model additions (proposed)
Per Memory item: . memory_id . text (or structured content) . embedding . topic_id (nullable until assigned) . importance . stability . keep . created_at . last_seen_at (last time it was referenced or matched) . last_retrieved_at (optional) . reinforcement_count (how many times this concept repeats) . duplicate_of (points to canonical memory if this item is a duplicate) . canonical_id (optional pointer to canonical summary memory) . superseded_by and supersedes (for decision revisions) . tombstone_state (soft delete marker with retention window)
Per Topic (optional but recommended for cheap organization): . topic_id . label (optional human-readable) . embedding_centroid . created_at, last_seen_at . doc_count, recent_doc_count . theme_score or stability_hint (optional)
Key mechanism 1: Repetition as reinforcement (brain-like strengthening)
Repetition should strengthen the underlying concept, not create 10 strong copies.
Policy: . When a new memory N matches an existing concept cluster: . Identify canonical memory C for that concept. . Reinforce C: . increase C.importance (bounded) . increase C.stability (bounded) . C.reinforcement_count += 1 . update C.last_seen_at = now . Mark N as redundant: . set N.duplicate_of = C . set low N.stability (it is not the authoritative version) . optionally store only a short stub for N
Rationale: . This approximates “I saw it 10 times so it sticks” while keeping storage compact.
Recommended reinforcement shape: . Use saturating growth with diminishing returns so the first few repeats matter most and it does not explode to 1.0 instantly. . Track two reinforcement modes: . burst_reinforcement: repeats in a short window, mostly indicates redundancy. . spaced_reinforcement: repeats across days or weeks, indicates true theme strength. . Spaced reinforcement should raise stability more than burst reinforcement.
Key mechanism 2: Dedup as a stability signal and as storage control
Dedup can be done cheaply without LLM scanning everything.
Cheap candidate generation: . Use embeddings plus topic grouping: . search for nearest neighbors within the same topic_id, or within top-k closest topic centroids . identify pairs or clusters above a similarity threshold . Optionally use cheap fingerprints for near-exact duplicates: . simhash or similar normalized-text fingerprint . Output a short list of candidate duplicate clusters for deep sleep processing.
Deep sleep semantic resolution: For each duplicate cluster: . Select canonical memory: . usually the highest stability, or the newest decision-like item . Merge or link: . create or update canonical summary memory if needed . mark others duplicate_of = canonical . Adjust stability: . canonical gets stronger (reinforcement) . duplicates get weaker, and eventually become eligible for tombstone or deletion . Preserve references: . if other memories point to a duplicate, either rewrite the pointer to the canonical or keep a minimal stub.
Key mechanism 3: Supersession and revision handling
Not every “duplicate topic” is a duplicate memory. Some are revisions.
Rule: . If the new memory materially changes a decision, constraint, or definition, treat it as a revision: . new memory becomes canonical revision . old canonical gets superseded_by = new . stability transfers forward to the new canonical (the concept remains strong, the old phrasing decays)
This allows the system to keep current truth while still acknowledging prior context.
Retention policy using importance and stability
Example thresholds (tunable): . keep = true: never delete; may summarize if explicitly allowed, but default is preserve. . stability >= 0.75: retain full fidelity; only compress lightly. . 0.50 <= stability < 0.75: summarize or compact after time; keep canonical, degrade duplicates faster. . stability < 0.50: eligible for removal after time, unless recently retrieved or referenced.
Recommended additional gates: . Retrieval protection: if recently retrieved or referenced, do not delete even if low stability. . Dependency protection: never delete a memory that is referenced unless the reference is migrated. . Cluster safety: never delete the last memory in a concept cluster without a canonical replacement summary.
Tombstone buffer to prevent irreversible mistakes
Deletion should be a two-step process: . Tombstone: remove full text, keep minimal stub (id, timestamp, topic, canonical pointer, 1-line summary). . Hard delete: only after one additional deep sleep cycle and only if still unused and unreferenced.
Implementation strategy (low risk incremental)

Phase 2: Link-only dedup . On ingest or light sleep: . if similarity hit, set duplicate_of and increment canonical reinforcement_count. . do not merge text yet.
Phase 3: Canonical consolidation . Weekly deep sleep: . summarize top clusters into canonicals . tombstone older duplicates
Phase 4: Full policy tuning . Add superseded detection, contradiction checks, theme scoring, and more advanced stability updates.
Expected outcomes
. Memory growth slows dramatically because repeated ideas reinforce canonicals instead of creating full copies. . Retrieval improves because the system surfaces a small set of canonical memories per concept. . Long-term forgetting becomes controlled, explainable, and reversible. . System behavior becomes closer to biological selection: fast tagging plus slow consolidation.
