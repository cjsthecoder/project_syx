*** ASSISTANT (data-message-author-role: assistant) ***
You can get that scene with *very* different sized black holes, depending on how you stage it.

The key idea:  
A black hole does not “suck” anything in at a distance. Its pull at a given distance is just normal gravity:

\[
g(r) = \frac{GM}{r^2}
\]

So there are two main ways to have a person get dragged in:

1. **They get dangerously close to a small or medium black hole**
2. **They stay relatively far, but the black hole is absurdly massive**

For drama, you almost always want case 1.

---

## 1. What does “powerful enough to suck a person in” actually mean?

You get that “oh no I am being pulled in” feeling when:

- The gravitational acceleration is stronger than what they can resist (thrusters, tethers, jetpacks, etc.)
- And/or the **tidal forces** (gravity difference between head and feet) are strong enough to yank them off a structure or tear them apart.

Tidal acceleration over a person’s height \(L \sim 2 \text{ m}\):

\[
a_{\text{tidal}} \approx \frac{2GM L}{r^3}
\]

Roughly, you get:

- “I am being pulled off my feet / my line snaps” when tidal forces or net acceleration reach a few g’s
- “Spaghettification” at much higher tidal accelerations

So at what distance from the horizon is that true?

---

## 2. Near a stellar mass black hole: great for your scene

Take a 10‑solar‑mass black hole (typical stellar remnant):

- Schwarzschild radius \(R_s \approx 30\) km

If a person is, say, at:

- \(r \sim 3 R_s \approx 90\) km from the center (so 60 km above the horizon)

You can already have:

- Gravity strong enough that:
  - They cannot escape without serious thrust
  - Small changes in velocity send them plunging inward
- Tidal forces big enough to:
  - Yank them off a hull if their tether fails
  - Give that “stretched” visceral description as they fall closer

That is a good story zone: close enough for drama, not instantly lethal.

**So for a gripping sequence:**

- Black hole size: **stellar mass** (few to ~10 solar masses, \(R_s \sim 9\)–30 km)
- Person is:
  - On a damaged ship in a decaying orbit at ~3–5 \(R_s\)
  - Or on a tethered platform being pulled inward as control thrusters fail
- The gravity well feels like a “sucking pit” because:
  - Orbital velocity is huge
  - Escape velocity is close to light speed
  - One misstep and they cross the point where no realistic thrust can save them

Visually and emotionally, it *absolutely* reads as “sucked in.”

---

- Local gravity and tidal pull spike
- Their tether snaps or anchor point shears
- They feel themselves yanked outward/downward toward the hole, stars warping and redshifting as they go
