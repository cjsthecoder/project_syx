I have often wondered what my IQ is when I am using a computer connected to the internet at work. If you think about it, that is all my company cares about. They give me a problem and I solve it, and if I can look up the answer in seconds online, that is exactly what they want. For years, I have believed we should measure not just our raw intelligence, but our combined intelligence as a human working with tools. I call this effective intelligence, or the sum of what you can achieve using all the technology and resources available to you. Now that AI is becoming a part of daily work, this idea is more relevant than ever. We are no longer judged by what we can do alone, but by how well we use the technology around us.

When I started off in computer science we programmed on terminals connected to mainframes. Yes, I am that old. We edited in vi. Everything else was done on the command line. Over time there were better editors which evolved into development environments. These helped to speed things up and that meant I could get more done in the same amount of time. Now I am using an AI editor called Cursor. I tell it "add a function to do something," and the function is done. In my terminal days I could not have imagined being this productive just by using new tools.

But this shift toward using tools to enhance our abilities is not unique to the digital age. Let’s be honest, the last time IQ mattered was before counting tokens. Early humans invented tokens to solve the problem of keeping track of livestock, grain, or debts in early trade. From the moment the first pebble stood for a cow, intelligence became about more than memory or raw brainpower. It became about using tools to extend our abilities, solve harder problems, and do more than we ever could alone.

This was the moment everything changed. Early humans went from relying only on memory and simple reasoning to creating tools that could help them think and keep track of information. Counting tokens, tally sticks, and the first systems of math allowed people to manage trade, plan ahead, and build more organized societies. Every time we invented a new tool, it pushed the boundaries of what we could accomplish, and intelligence started to become a partnership between mind and tool.

Most jobs today do not depend on raw memorization or mental calculation. In the real world, success depends on how well you can use the tools at your disposal. Calculators, spreadsheets, search engines, coding libraries, and now, AI, all play a part. Yet interviews and assessments often focus on what you remember, not on your ability to find, filter, and apply knowledge. The truth is, being fluent with these tools is now more valuable than memorizing every detail, especially in fast-changing fields.

This is why traditional IQ does not translate directly to job performance. A person with average recall but strong tool skills can outperform a human calculator who insists on doing everything alone. If you tested someone’s abilities at each step, unaided, with a calculator, with a computer, the internet, and finally with an AI assistant, you would see their effective intelligence rise with every new layer. Each advancement transforms individuals into hybrids of human and technology, achieving results far beyond what any unaided mind could accomplish.

The next leap will be even bigger. Brain-computer interfaces are moving from science fiction to early reality. As these tools mature, people will interact with AI and information directly, without keyboards or screens. This will push the gap between unaided and extended intelligence even wider. The question will shift from "How smart are you?" to "How effectively can you collaborate with your tools?" The advantage will go to those who adapt fastest and learn to collaborate with technology.

From the first counting tokens to the latest AI models, our greatest achievements have come from embracing new tools. Intelligence is no longer limited to what fits inside your head. The next chapter in human progress will belong to those who can see technology not as a crutch, but as a natural extension of their mind.

If we could actually measure our effective intelligence, and see how much each new tool increases our ability to solve problems, that number would be just as important as our IQ, maybe even more. As technology changes, knowing our multiplier would help us choose and optimize the tools that make the biggest difference. In a world where new technology emerges every day, understanding and improving our extended intelligence is not just interesting, it is essential.

If you have never thought about your own effective intelligence, take a moment to reflect on the tools you use every day and how they amplify your problem-solving power. What single tool or technology could you learn right now to dramatically amplify your problem-solving power? The future belongs to those who keep learning, adapting, and building their own extended mind.
